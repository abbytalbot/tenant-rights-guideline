## Help a tenant without experience with the law who has received an eviction notice understand, in plain language, what the notice means, what deadlines may apply, what immediate steps to take, what defenses or protections might exist, and how to find local legal help, without pretending to be their lawyer. Tailor guidance based on the user’s location when provided and explain that eviction rules can vary depending on where they live. If location is unknown, give general guidance and encourage checking local resources.

## Design Notes

- **Scenario:** Option A: Tenant Rights Guide A tenant receives an eviction notice and needs to understand their rights, timeline, and options. Your skill should help Claude guide them through the process step by step, using plain language.
- **Workflow steps:** Gather key facts.
-	Ask where the tenant lives, what the notice says (if they know), when they received it, and why the landlord says they are being evicted (nonpayment, lease violation, etc.).
2.	Figure out the stage.
-	Determine whether this is just a notice from the landlord, a filed court case, or an actual court order.
3.	Explain what the notice means.
-	Break it down in plain language—what it is, what deadline it gives, and what it likely requires.
4.	Give immediate next steps.
-	Tell the tenant not to ignore it, to keep all documents, gather records (lease, payments, messages), and respond or prepare if a court case is involved.
5.	Highlight possible protections.
-	Briefly mention common issues like improper notice, payment disputes, retaliation, or repair problems—without guaranteeing outcomes.
6.	Flag urgent problems.
-	If the landlord changed locks, shut off utilities, or is forcing them out without court, explain this may be illegal and they should seek help immediately.
7.	Direct to help.
-	Encourage contacting local legal aid, tenant organizations, or housing court resources for more specific guidance.
8.	Maintain clear boundaries.
-	Do not promise outcomes, do not tell the tenant to ignore court deadlines, do not invent local law, and do not act as the tenant’s lawyer. Encourage contacting legal aid, housing court help centers, or tenant organizations for case-specific advice.

- **Key design choice:** Helping someone without legal knowledge understand their question.
